# SPEED FORGE

**Version:** 1.0  
**Created:** March 5, 2026  
**Author:** Mahmoud  
**Purpose:** Systematic speed optimization for competition math at every level

## The Core Idea

Speed in competition math isn't about thinking faster. It's about recognizing faster. The student who solves AIME P10 in 12 minutes instead of 25 isn't computing faster — they identified the technique chain in 30 seconds instead of 8 minutes, then executed a proof architecture they've run dozens of times before.

Speed training must be built into the system from the start, not bolted on at the end. The Speed Forge alternates between deep understanding and fast retrieval so that every technique you learn is both deeply understood AND rapidly accessible under pressure.

---

## The Two Components of Solve Time

Every problem has two time components:

**Recognition time:** Reading the problem → identifying the technique/architecture.
"This is a counting problem using complementary counting with modular reduction."

**Execution time:** Identified technique → completed solution.
The actual computation, algebraic manipulation, or proof writing.

Track these SEPARATELY for every timed problem. The ratio tells you where you're losing time:

| Pattern | Diagnosis | Fix |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| Slow recognition, fast execution | You know the techniques but can't see through costumes | More OOB drills, more Identify-Only from Chain Training |
| Fast recognition, slow execution | You see the technique but can't deploy it smoothly | More Forge compression ladders, computation drilling |
| Slow both | You don't know the technique well enough yet | Back to depth work — this problem is above your current level |
| Fast both | This problem is below your training level | Move to harder problems |

## The Three-Day Cycle

The optimal structure alternates between three modes:

### Day A — Speed Day

Problems you SHOULD be able to solve at your current level, done under aggressive time pressure. The goal is retrieval speed, not new learning.

**AMC level:** 5-8 problems, 3-5 minutes each. These should be problems where you know the technique — the challenge is accessing it fast.

**AIME level:** 4-6 problems, 8-15 minutes each. Single-technique problems where the technique is known but the costume varies.

**USAJMO+ level:** 2-3 problems, 20-30 minutes each. Problems where you've seen the proof architecture before and need to deploy it under pressure.

If you can't solve a Speed Day problem within the time limit, it reveals a gap — either the technique isn't as solid as you thought, or the problem is above your current speed level. Flag it and move it to the next Depth Day.

### Day B — Depth Day

Problems at or slightly above your current level, done with NO time limit. The goal is new understanding, not speed. This is standard Forge work — attempt, extract atoms, build trigger sentences.

Take as long as you need. 45 minutes on one problem is fine. 90 minutes is fine. The point is understanding the technique deeply enough that it becomes Speed Day material in the future.

**This is where new techniques enter your system.** Speed Day only uses techniques you already know. Depth Day is where you learn new ones.

### Day C — Transfer Day

Yesterday's Depth Day problems, now under time pressure. This is the critical bridge — you take something you understood deeply yesterday and train yourself to access it fast today.

**The protocol:**

1. Read only the problem statement (no notes from yesterday)
2. Solve under time pressure (set timer to 60% of what it took you yesterday)
3. After solving, compare with yesterday's solution
4. Write trigger sentence focusing on speed: "The fast path to this was [X] because [feature] should have immediately signaled [technique]"

If you can't solve it in 60% of yesterday's time, the technique hasn't consolidated enough. It goes back to Depth for one more day before returning to Transfer.

## The Rotation — Structure Over Topic

Don't rotate by topic (NT → Geo → Algebra). Rotate by PROBLEM STRUCTURE:

| Day | Structure | What It Trains |
|-----|-----------|---------------|
| Monday | Direct computation / formula application | Raw technique speed |
| Tuesday | Casework / systematic enumeration | Organizational speed |
| Wednesday | Complementary counting / indirect approaches | Reframing speed |
| Thursday | Technique chaining (2-3 techniques combined) | Transition speed |
| Friday | Estimation / bounding / inequality | Approximation speed |

Each day, the problems come from DIFFERENT topics but share the same underlying structure. Monday might have an algebra problem, a geometry problem, and a number theory problem — all requiring direct formula application. This trains cross-domain recognition: you stop seeing "algebra problem" and start seeing "direct computation problem wearing an algebra costume."

### Advanced Rotation (USAJMO+ level, 2028+)

| Day | Architecture | What It Trains |
|-----|-------------|---------------|
| Monday | Invariant / monovariant proofs | Seeing what doesn't change |
| Tuesday | Construction / existence proofs | Building objects with properties |
| Wednesday | Extremal principle / optimization | Seeing why min/max matters |
| Thursday | Induction family (standard, strong, descent) | Recursive structure recognition |
| Friday | Contradiction / impossibility proofs | Seeing why something can't work |

## Phase-Specific Speed Training

### Phase 1: AMC Speed (Now → November 2026)

AMC 10 gives you 75 minutes for 25 problems. Speed is THE primary bottleneck.

**Target pacing:**
- Problems 1-10: 2-3 minutes each (20-30 min total)
- Problems 11-15: 3-4 minutes each (15-20 min total)  
- Problems 16-20: 4-5 minutes each (16-20 min total)
- Problems 21-25: remaining time, attempt 2-3

**Speed drills:**

**The 1-10 Blitz:** Take problems 1-10 from a past AMC 10. Set timer for 20 minutes. Solve all 10. If you can't finish in 20 minutes, your foundations have gaps — specific problems you missed reveal which Intro topics need reinforcement.

Do this 3x per week. Track your time weekly. The goal: 10/10 correct in under 20 minutes consistently. When you can do this reliably on any AMC 10 from the last 10 years, problems 1-10 are automatic and you've freed 10+ minutes for the harder problems.

**The 16-20 Sprint:** Take problems 16-20 from a past AMC 10. Set timer for 20 minutes. These are the problems where speed separates a 120 from a 135. Track recognition time versus execution time for each one.

**The Strategic Skip Drill:** Full AMC 10 under time. But the goal isn't maximum score — it's optimal time allocation. After finishing, analyze: did you spend 8 minutes on a problem you should have skipped? Did you leave a problem blank that you could have solved in the time you wasted elsewhere? Strategic skipping is a speed skill.

**AMC-specific speed insight:** On AMC, the scoring is 6 points correct, 1.5 points blank, 0 points wrong. Leaving a problem blank when you're less than ~40% confident is BETTER than guessing. Speed training for AMC includes training your confidence calibration — knowing when to attempt, when to skip, and when to guess with 30 seconds left.

### Phase 2: AIME Speed (December 2026 → February 2027)

AIME gives you 3 hours for 15 problems. No penalty for wrong answers.

**Target pacing:**
- Problems 1-5: 6-8 minutes each (30-40 min total)
- Problems 6-9: 12-15 minutes each (48-60 min total)
- Problems 10-12: 15-20 minutes each (45-60 min total)
- Problems 13-15: remaining time, attempt 1-2

**Speed drills:**

**The P1-5 Kill Chain:** Take problems 1-5 from a past AIME. Set timer for 30 minutes. These should be nearly automatic. Track which ones take more than 8 minutes — those reveal technique gaps at the Intermediate level.

Do this 2x per week during AIME prep. The goal: 5/5 correct in under 30 minutes consistently.

**The P6-9 Recognition Sprint:** Take problems 6-9 from a past AIME. Don't solve them fully. Just identify the technique chain for each one within 3 minutes per problem. Write: "This uses [technique A] → [technique B] → [computation]." Check against the solution. This is pure recognition training — Chain Training Drill Type 1 applied to AIME.

**The Computation Accuracy Drill:** Solve an AIME problem fully. Then solve it AGAIN using a different method. If you get the same answer, high confidence. If different, find the error. This takes extra time in practice but prevents the single arithmetic error that costs you a full point on the real test.

**AIME-specific speed insight:** On AIME, there's no penalty for wrong answers and answers are integers 000-999. With 5 minutes left, guess on every unsolved problem. The expected value of a random guess is 1/1000 of a point — essentially zero cost. But sometimes you've narrowed the answer to "probably between 100 and 200 and probably even" — that partial knowledge makes a last-minute guess worthwhile.

### Phase 3: USAJMO/USAMO Speed (Spring 2027 → 2029)

4.5 hours for 3 problems per day. Speed here means triage speed and proof writing speed, not computation speed.

**Target pacing:**
- First 15-20 minutes: read all 3 problems, classify each one, choose which to attempt first
- Next 2-3 hours: primary problem — complete proof
- Remaining time: second problem — as much progress as possible
- Last 30 min: clean up, check for gaps, write partial results on third problem

**Speed drills:**

**The Triage Sprint:** Take 3 USAJMO problems. Read all three. In 10 minutes, write for each: probable architecture, estimated difficulty for you personally, and your attempt order. Check against actual solutions — were your classifications correct?

The goal: your triage accuracy should be above 70%. If you consistently misjudge which problem is easiest for you, you're wasting contest time on the wrong problem.

**The Skeleton Sprint:** Take a USAJMO problem you've already solved in depth. Set timer for 15 minutes. Write ONLY the proof skeleton — claim graph, key lemmas stated without proof, finish structure. Not a full proof — just the architecture.

This trains the specific skill of getting your structural understanding onto paper fast. On the real test, having a correct skeleton in 15 minutes means you spend the remaining 2+ hours filling in details rather than searching for direction.

**The Clean Write Drill:** Take a proof you've already fully worked out. Rewrite it from scratch as fast as possible in clean contest prose. Time yourself. The goal: a 7/7 proof that took you 3 hours to discover should take under 45 minutes to write cleanly once you know the argument.

This separates discovery speed from writing speed. If your writing speed is slow, you might discover a solution with 90 minutes left but not finish writing it — that's tragic and trainable.

### Phase 4: IMO Speed (MOP level, 2028-2030)

4.5 hours for 3 problems. The triage is even more critical because the difficulty spread is enormous (P1 might be solvable, P3 might be impossible for you).

**Speed drills:**

**The Architecture Library Sprint:** 6 IMO Shortlist problems, 5 minutes each. For each one: identify the proof architecture, write the first three lines of the approach, stop. Check against solutions. This is OOB drilling at the highest level.

**The Re-derive Sprint:** Take an IMO problem you solved (or studied) last week. Re-derive the full solution from memory under time pressure. Set the timer to 50% of your original solve time. If you can re-derive in half the time, the technique is truly internalized. If not, it needs more consolidation.

**The Cross-Domain Sprint:** 4 problems, all using the same proof architecture, from 4 different topics. Solve all 4 under combined time pressure. This trains the realization that "this geometry problem and this number theory problem are the same proof" — the ultimate speed skill.

## The Recheck Protocol

Every problem solved on Speed Day or Transfer Day gets rechecked the next day:

**Step 1:** Read only the problem statement. No notes.

**Step 2:** Write the proof architecture from memory. Not the full solution — just the skeleton. "Use complementary counting. Total is C(n,k). Subtract cases where [constraint violated]. Reduction via modular arithmetic to get answer mod 1000."

**Step 3:** Check skeleton against yesterday's full solution.

| Result | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Skeleton correct and complete | Technique consolidated. Move on. |
| Skeleton mostly correct, minor gap | Note the gap. It'll likely self-correct. Recheck in 3 days. |
| Skeleton wrong or major gap | Technique hasn't stuck. Back to Depth Day. Returns to Transfer in 2 days. |
| Can't remember the problem at all | Technique was never learned, only performed. Full re-do on next Depth Day. |

## Thought Process Recording

During Speed Day, record your thought process in real time. Quick voice notes or scribbled annotations:

"See symmetric equation... thinking Vieta's... no, the constraint is on sums not products... switching to generating functions... got it, coefficient extraction."

Review these recordings on Recheck Day. Look for meta-patterns:

| Meta-Pattern | What It Means | Fix |
|--------------|--------------|-----|
| "I always try algebra first" | Topic bias — you default to your strongest area | Force yourself to classify by structure before attempting |
| "I spend 5 min computing before checking for shortcuts" | Execution-first habit | Add a mandatory 60-second pause for structural assessment before computing anything |
| "I switch approaches 3 times per problem" | Weak initial classification | More OOB drills, more Identify-Only practice |
| "I get the technique right but make arithmetic errors" | Execution fragility under time pressure | Dedicated computation accuracy drills |

These meta-patterns are where the biggest speed gains hide. Fixing one meta-pattern can save 2-3 minutes per problem across the board.

## The Compression Metric

Track weekly averages for each problem difficulty level:

**For AMC level:**
- Average time for P1-10 (target: 2 min)
- Average time for P11-15 (target: 3.5 min)
- Average time for P16-20 (target: 4.5 min)
- Hit rate for P21-25 (target: 50%+ of attempted)

**For AIME level:**
- Average time for P1-5 (target: 6 min)
- Average time for P6-9 (target: 12 min)
- Average recognition time for P10-12 (target: under 3 min)
- Average total time for P10-12 (target: 18 min)

**For USAJMO+ level:**
- Average triage time for 3 problems (target: 15 min)
- Average skeleton time (target: 20 min)
- Average full proof time for a problem at your level (target: 90 min)
- Clean rewrite time (target: 40 min)

Plot these weekly. The curves should trend downward. Plateaus lasting more than 3 weeks indicate you need either harder problems (if the plateau is near your target) or a technique gap to fill (if the plateau is above your target).

## Integration with Other Systems

### With Family Forge
- Depth Days feed new techniques into the Forge
- Speed Days test whether Forge families have achieved compression
- If a forged technique is slow on Speed Day, the compression ladder needs more reps

### With Chain Training
- Chain Training Drill Type 1 (Identify Only) IS the recognition component of Speed Day
- Speed Day adds the execution component on top of Chain Training's recognition training
- The P6-9 Recognition Sprint is literally Chain Training applied to AIME

### With OOB (Opening Book)
- OOB drills build the pattern library that Speed Day tests
- Speed Day recognition times directly measure OOB effectiveness
- If recognition is slow, add more OOB cards for that problem type

### With ETOH / Error Analysis
- Speed Day errors get tagged K/P/E/T as usual
- Add a new tag: **S (Speed error)** — you knew the technique, your recognition was fast, but time pressure caused a computational error you wouldn't have made untimed
- S errors indicate you're pushing speed beyond your current execution reliability — either slow down slightly or drill computation accuracy

### With the Parallel Problem Protocol
- When a Speed Day problem stumps you, don't look at the solution immediately
- Instead, use the Parallel Problem Protocol: diagnose why you're stuck (Track A/B/C/D) and get a simpler parallel problem
- This preserves the learning value of the problem instead of burning it by reading the answer

## When to Start

**Right now:** AMC Speed drills. The P1-10 Blitz requires nothing beyond Intro series knowledge. Start doing these 2-3x per week alongside your AoPS chapter work. They take 20-30 minutes and directly prepare you for November.

**Summer 2026:** Add AIME Speed drills once you've started Intermediate work and have exposure to AIME problems.

**Fall 2027:** Add USAJMO Speed drills once Proof Gym is established and you have proof-writing fluency.

**2028+:** Add IMO-level Speed drills once you're working at Shortlist difficulty.

Speed training scales with your level. Start now at AMC. The habits — tracking recognition time, rechecking architectures, recording thought processes — transfer directly to every higher level.

---

## The Meta-Principle

Speed is not the opposite of depth. Speed is depth that has been compressed through repetition until it's automatic. The fastest solvers aren't rushing. They're calm. They see the structure instantly because they've seen it hundreds of times before. The speed comes from recognition, not from hurrying.

Train depth first. Then compress depth into speed. Then use speed to free time for deeper problems. The cycle compounds forever.
