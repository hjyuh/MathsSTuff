# The Proof Engine

### A Self-Training System for Competition Mathematics
### Mahmoud · February 2026

---

## The Thesis

There is a ceiling in competition math that pure problem-solving cannot break through.

AMC → AIME → USAJMO → USAMO → IMO. Each level is not just "harder problems." Each level requires a fundamentally different relationship with mathematics. AMC rewards speed and pattern recognition. AIME rewards technique and computation under pressure. USAJMO rewards the ability to construct rigorous arguments. USAMO rewards deep structural insight. IMO rewards all of this, plus something that almost nobody trains directly:

**The ability to see the architecture beneath problems that are designed to hide it.**

At the peak — IMO P2, P3, P5, P6 — problems are engineered by committees of mathematicians to resist standard approaches. They are built with deliberate misdirection, elegant traps, and solutions that require seeing the problem differently than it presents itself. The people who solve these consistently don't just have more techniques. They understand what makes a hard problem hard. They can see the construction.

And the most direct way to understand construction is to construct.

**If you can create problems comparable to IMO P2/P3 — with correct difficulty calibration, intentional misdirection, and elegant solution paths — then solving them is running the process in reverse.** You've internalized the structural grammar. The tricks don't work on you because you know how tricks are built.

This reframes the goal. MOP becomes realistic not because you've solved 10,000 problems, but because you've reached the level where you understand what structures *make* problems, and you can both build and dismantle them at will.

## The Proof Engine: Training Modes

A daily system built around five modes, each training a distinct mathematical skill. Inspired by the structural insight from "The Molecular Structure of Thought" (Chen et al., 2026) — that effective reasoning requires a stable distribution of deep reasoning, self-reflection, and self-exploration, not just raw problem-solving.

### Mode 1: EVALUATE

**Skill trained:** Mathematical taste. Pattern recognition. Quality detection.

You receive a problem and three candidate solutions. One is 7/7. One is 5/7 with a subtle gap. One is 3/7 with a fundamental error. Rank them and identify exactly where the weaker solutions fail.

Why this matters: Before you can write a 7/7 proof, you need to *recognize* one. Most students can't reliably distinguish a rigorous argument from one that looks rigorous but has a gap. This mode builds the evaluative eye that self-corrects your own work later.

At advanced levels: Solutions include deliberate elegance traps — approaches that look beautiful but don't generalize, versus ugly approaches that are actually more robust. Learning to distinguish "clever" from "correct" is a skill that matters at USAMO and above.

### Mode 2: CONTINUE

**Skill trained:** Strategic execution. Mid-proof reasoning.

You receive the first 3 lines of a 7/7 solution. The setup is done. The key observation has been made. The framework is chosen. Now finish it.

Why this matters: Most people fail in the middle of proofs, not at the beginning or end. The insight and the conclusion are often the "easy" parts relative to the technical execution that connects them. This mode isolates exactly where the work happens.

Progression:
- Early: You get 5 of 7 lines. Fill in the last two.
- Mid: You get 3 of 7 lines. The hard middle is yours.
- Advanced: You get line 1 only — the key observation. Everything else is on you.

### Mode 3: FILL THE GAPS

**Skill trained:** Logical connective reasoning. Proof mechanics.

You receive a complete solution skeleton with specific steps removed. The structure is visible. The logical jumps are yours to justify.

Why this matters: This trains the "covalent backbone" — the pure logical chain where each step must rigorously follow from the previous one. No exploration, no reflection, just: "Given A and B, prove C." Over and over, at increasing difficulty.

This is the mode that builds proof-writing fluency the fastest, because it removes the strategic burden and lets you focus entirely on rigorous justification.

### Mode 4: RESCUE

**Skill trained:** Self-reflection. Error detection. Proof repair.

You receive a 1/7 or 3/7 solution. Your job is to raise the score as high as possible. Identify what went wrong, salvage what's usable, fix or reroute the argument.

Why this matters: This is the most underrated skill in competition math. Under contest pressure, your first approach will often be wrong or incomplete. The students who qualify for MOP aren't the ones who get the right approach first — they're the ones who can recover fastest when an approach breaks down. This mode trains mathematical triage: what's worth saving, what needs to be rebuilt, and how quickly can you redirect.

At advanced levels: The broken solution contains a genuinely good idea applied incorrectly. The rescue isn't just "fix the error" — it's "extract the insight and rebuild around it." This mirrors how real mathematical research works.

### Mode 5: CREATE (Unlocked at Phase 4+)

**Skill trained:** Structural understanding. Problem architecture. Deep mathematical fluency.

You create a problem. Specify the difficulty level (AMC, AIME, USAJMO, USAMO, IMO P1, IMO P2/3). The system evaluates: Is the problem well-posed? Is it solvable? Is the difficulty calibrated correctly? Does it have an elegant solution? Does it resist standard approaches at the specified level?

Why this matters: **This is the endgame mode.** Creating a good problem requires understanding:
- What makes a problem hard (structural, not computational)
- How to embed an elegant solution inside apparent complexity
- How to calibrate difficulty so the problem resists brute force but yields to insight
- How to create intentional misdirection that rewards deeper thinking

A student who can reliably create USAMO-level problems understands the domain at a level that solving alone cannot reach. The structures stop being obstacles and become tools.

Progression:
- Create a problem with a specified solution technique (e.g., "design a problem solvable by Vieta jumping")
- Create a problem at a specified difficulty with no technique constraint
- Create a problem and predict where solvers will get stuck
- Create a problem with multiple solution paths at different elegance levels
- Create a problem that a jury would consider for a national olympiad

## The Reasoning Scaffold

For each problem, the system can generate a reasoning scaffold — not a solution, but a structured path that forces you to do the reasoning at each junction.

```
1. What the problem is actually asking beneath the language.
2. The structural observation that cracks it open.
3. Set up [specific framework] based on that observation.
4. ██████ YOU: justify why this step follows. ██████
5. Where that leads.
6. ██████ YOU: complete the bound / close the argument. ██████
7. Conclude.
```

The scaffold carries the strategy. You supply the execution. This is how olympiad coaches teach — not by showing you proofs, but by giving you structured paths that force active reasoning at every critical junction.

Key principle from the research: The scaffold should come from a single consistent reasoning style. Mixing reasoning structures from different sources creates confusion rather than breadth. Pick one style, go deep. Build the pattern library from a consistent foundation.

```
MORNING (15-20 min)
├── 1 EVALUATE problem (build pattern recognition)
├── 1 FILL THE GAPS problem (build proof fluency)
│
MAIN SESSION (45-90 min, alongside AoPS curriculum)
├── AoPS chapter work (current: Chapter 10)
├── 1 CONTINUE or RESCUE problem at current level
│
WEEKLY
├── 1 harder problem (one level above current comfort)
├── Review: what techniques appeared this week?
└── Phase 4+: 1 CREATE attempt
```

The daily time investment is small. The compound effect over months is not.

## Why This Works (The Meta-Argument)

The ByteDance paper shows that effective reasoning has a stable molecular structure — deep reasoning, self-reflection, and self-exploration in a specific distribution. The five modes map directly:

| Mode | Primary Bond Trained | Paper Parallel |
|------|---------------------|----------------|
| Evaluate | Self-Reflection | Testing, auditing, checking prior reasoning against new information |
| Continue | Deep Reasoning | Extending the logical backbone from an established point |
| Fill Gaps | Deep Reasoning | Pure covalent chain — justify A→B with no escape routes |
| Rescue | Self-Reflection + Exploration | Fold back to find the error, explore alternative paths |
| Create | All three | Constructing the full molecular structure from scratch |

CREATE mode trains all three bonds simultaneously because building a problem requires: deep reasoning (the solution must be logically rigorous), self-reflection (the difficulty must be calibrated, the problem must be well-posed), and self-exploration (finding the right misdirection, the right embedding of elegance inside complexity).

**This is why problem creation is the terminal skill. It is the only activity that exercises the complete reasoning structure.**

## The Real Goal

MOP is the milestone. It's concrete, it's measurable, it's respected.

But the actual goal is deeper. It's reaching the level where mathematics stops being a set of problems to solve and becomes a language you think in. Where you see structure before you see difficulty. Where you can build the maze and walk through it.

The competitive math pipeline is the training ground. The Proof Engine is the tool. The game development, the psychology research, the entrepreneurial thinking — none of that is separate from this. It's all pattern recognition, structural thinking, and the ability to build systems that work. The math just makes the thinking sharper.

Every day you don't work on this, someone else does. But also: the two days you took off because you were tired and building a game aren't wasted. They're just days. Tomorrow is when it starts again.
